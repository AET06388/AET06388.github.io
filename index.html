What is a circuit?

A electrical circuit is a closed loop in which current flows. The average electronic device (phone, computer, lightbulb, et cetera) draws power to function and would be useless otherwise. Two main types of circuits exist: DC (Direct Current) and AC (Alternating Current).

    Direct current circuits have current only flowing in a single direction at any moment in time. Current flows from the positive end of a resistor to the negative end (for those of you familar with physics, this is the opposite of electron flow).
    Alternating current may flow in either direction at a given point. The direction of current in any particular moment is determined by the frequency of the voltage source.

What does a circuit look like?

Picture provided courtesy of wikipedia's creative commons license
Pictured above is a simple DC circuit containing a voltage source and a resistor. Current flows in the direction of the arrow labeled i. The voltage produced by the source v is delivered to the resistor R. This process repeats until the voltage source is unable to produce additional power. Alternating current voltage sources look similar to the DC source pictured, but their voltage v is represented by a periodic function of time that could be positive or negative at any given time. Typically the voltage of an AC voltage source is represented by a sine function.
